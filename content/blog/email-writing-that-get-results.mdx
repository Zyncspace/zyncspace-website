<p>The average professional receives 121 emails per day and sends about 40. With inboxes overflowing, how do you make your messages stand out? The answer lies in understanding that email is a conversation—not a document dump.</p>

            <blockquote>"Email is a wonderful thing for people whose job is to fill other people's inboxes. It's a terrible thing for people whose job is to do actual work." — Leo Babauta</blockquote>

            <h2>The Problem with Most Emails</h2>
            <p>Most emails fail because they:</p>
            <ul>
              <li>Have vague or missing subject lines</li>
              <li>Buried the main request deep in the text</li>
              <li>Included unnecessary information</li>
              <li>Lacked clear next steps</li>
              <li>Were written from the sender's perspective, not the reader's</li>
            </ul>

            <h2>The PEEL Framework for Effective Emails</h2>

            <h3>Purpose</h3>
            <p>Start every email by stating your purpose clearly. One sentence should answer: Why am I writing this email?</p>

            <h3>Essential Information</h3>
            <p>Include only what the reader needs to respond. Cut anything that doesn't directly support your purpose.</p>

            <h3>Expectation</h3>
            <p>Be explicit about what you want. Do you need a decision? Feedback? Action? State it clearly and early.</p>

            <h3>Length</h3>
            <p>Respect the reader's time. Shorter emails get faster responses. If your email exceeds 5 paragraphs, consider if it should be a meeting.</p>

            <h2>Crafting the Perfect Subject Line</h2>
            <p>Your subject line is your first impression. It determines whether your email gets opened, ignored, or archived. A good subject line:</p>
            <ul>
              <li>Is specific: "Q4 budget approval needed by Friday" not "Budget"</li>
              <li>Creates urgency when appropriate: "[Urgent] Server down"</li>
              <li>Uses keywords the reader will search: Include project names, dates, or actions</li>
              <li>Avoids ALL CAPS and excessive punctuation</li>
            </ul>
