            <p class="lead">Feedback is the breakfast of champions—but only when it's delivered effectively. The difference between feedback that motivates and feedback that demoralizes lies not in what you say, but how you say it and the framework you use.</p>

            <h2>The SBI Model: Situation-Behavior-Impact</h2>
            <p>The SBI model, developed by Cisco, provides a structured approach to delivering behavioral feedback that is specific and non-judgmental.</p>

            <h3>Situation</h3>
            <p>Describe the specific context where you observed the behavior. This grounds the feedback in reality and makes it clear what you're referring to.</p>
            <p><em>"During yesterday's client presentation..."</em></p>

            <h3>Behavior</h3>
            <p>Describe the observable behavior—actions or words, not interpretations. Focus on what was said or done, not assumed intentions.</p>
            <p><em>"...you handled the client's difficult questions calmly and provided clear, data-backed responses..."</em></p>

            <h3>Impact</h3>
            <p>Explain the effect the behavior had on you, the team, or the project. This helps the recipient understand why the behavior matters.</p>
            <p><em>"...which helped build the client's confidence in our solution and contributed to closing the deal."</em></p>

            <h2>The COIN Model: Context-Observation-Impact-Next Steps</h2>
            <p>For feedback that requires action planning, the COIN model adds a crucial element: direction for improvement.</p>
            <ul>
              <li><strong>Context:</strong> Set the scene for the feedback</li>
              <li><strong>Observation:</strong> Describe what you saw or heard</li>
              <li><strong>Impact:</strong> Explain the effect</li>
              <li><strong>Next Steps:</strong> Specify what should happen differently</li>
            </ul>

            <h2>The Start-Stop-Continue Framework</h2>
            <p>This simple but powerful model works well for group feedback or regular check-ins:</p>
            <ul>
              <li><strong>Start:</strong> What new behaviors or practices should begin?</li>
              <li><strong>Stop:</strong> What should be discontinued?</li>
              <li><strong>Continue:</strong> What is working well and should persist?</li>
            </ul>

            <h2>The Sandwich Method: Proceed with Caution</h2>
            <p>You've likely heard of the "feedback sandwich"—positive comment, constructive criticism, positive comment. While well-intentioned, research suggests this approach can backfire. Recipients may dismiss both the criticism and the praise as insincere.</p>
            <p>If you use this method, ensure the positive feedback is genuine and specific, and that the constructive portion is the clear focus of the conversation.</p>

            <h2>Delivering Feedback Effectively</h2>
            <p>Even the best framework falls flat without proper delivery. Consider these principles:</p>

            <h3>Timing Matters</h3>
            <p>Provide feedback close to the event while details are fresh. Delayed feedback loses impact and specificity.</p>

            <h3>Create Psychological Safety</h3>
            <p>Frame feedback as an opportunity for growth, not punishment. The goal is development, not judgment.</p>

            <h3>Be Specific and Objective</h3>
            <p>Generic feedback ("good job" or "needs improvement") provides no actionable insight. Get specific about what worked or what needs to change.</p>

            <h3>Invite Dialogue</h3>
            <p>Feedback should be a conversation, not a monologue. Ask for the other person's perspective and listen actively to their response.</p>

            <h2>Receiving Feedback Gracefully</h2>
            <p>Giving feedback is only half the equation. Being able to receive it without defensiveness is equally important:</p>
            <ul>
              <li>Listen without interrupting</li>
              <li>Ask clarifying questions</li>
              <li>Thank the person for their input</li>
              <li>Reflect before responding</li>
              <li>Take action on what you can use</li>
            </ul>

            <h2>Building a Feedback Culture</h2>
            <p>The most successful teams normalize feedback at all levels. This means leaders model receiving feedback openly, peer-to-peer feedback is encouraged, and feedback is seen as a gift rather than criticism.</p>

            <h2>Conclusion</h2>
            <p>Feedback frameworks provide structure, but the art of feedback lies in genuine care for the recipient's growth. When delivered with empathy, specificity, and a true investment in the person's success, feedback becomes one of the most powerful tools in your leadership toolkit.</p>
